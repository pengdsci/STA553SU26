STA 553 DATA VISUALIZATION

Various Types of Visual Design Principles

🌙 Dark
🧠 1. Cognitive & Perceptual How we see
🔮 Gestalt Proximity
Objects placed close together are perceived as a group.
  • Group related bars in a chart; separate distinct categories with whitespace.
  • Use spatial clustering to reduce cognitive effort in dashboards.
  • Proximity overrides color – position is stronger than hue for grouping.
📌 Application: cluster KPIs by business unit, not randomly.
⚡ Pre-attentive Color
Hue & intensity spotted in <250ms. Highlight outliers instantly.
  • Use a single bright hue (red/orange) on a gray background to direct attention.
  • Limit pre-attentive markers to 1–2 per view to avoid dilution.
  • Shape and orientation are also pre-attentive; combine with color for accessibility.
🔴 Single red dot among gray ones draws immediate focus.
🧩 Miller’s Law (Chunking)
Working memory holds ~7±2 items. Limit palette to 5-7 colors.
  • Beyond 7 categories, group into "Other" or use hierarchical nesting.
  • Apply chunking to dashboard filters: present top 5, rest under "More".
  • Legends with >7 items cause constant eye travel; consider small multiples.
🎨 Palette strategy: 5 categorical hues + neutral gray for context.
🎨 2. Artistic & Aesthetic How we feel
🖋️ Data-Ink Ratio (Tufte)
Maximize data ink, erase non-data ink and redundant elements.
  • Remove heavy gridlines, 3D effects, and ornamental shadows.
  • Use subtle axis lines; let the data shape the visual weight.
  • High data-ink ratio improves clarity and speeds up interpretation.
📊 Erase borders, mute grids – every pixel should convey information.
🌸 Aesthetic-Usability Effect
Beautiful designs are perceived as easier to use and more trustworthy.
  • Consistent typography, harmonious whitespace, and polished color schemes increase engagement.
  • Even when functionality is identical, aesthetic dashboards receive higher user satisfaction scores.
  • Balance visual appeal with analytical integrity – avoid style over substance.
✨ Typography hierarchy + intentional color palette = credibility.
🎭 Emotional & Cultural Palette
Color evokes emotion and carries cultural meaning.
  • Warm palettes (reds, oranges) suggest urgency or passion; cool palettes convey calm and trust.
  • Respect cultural conventions: red = loss in Western finance, but prosperity in some Eastern contexts.
  • Use a muted background canvas to make the data colors feel more vibrant.
🔵 Finance: blue for trust; Environment: green/brown earth tones.
🏗️ 3. Structural & Composition How we organize
🗺️ Shneiderman’s Mantra
Overview first, zoom and filter, then details-on-demand.
  • Start with high-level KPIs; let users drill down interactively.
  • Provide clear navigation cues for filtering (click, brush, dropdown).
  • Tooltip details appear on hover, preserving the clean overview.
📈 Dashboard flow: summary tiles → trend charts → raw data table.
🪟 Small Multiples (Trellis)
Repeat the same chart structure for different categories.
  • All panels share identical axes and scales for instant comparison.
  • Reduces cognitive load compared to overlapping lines on a single chart.
  • Ideal for comparing trends across regions, products, or time periods.
📉 Grid of mini line charts, one per product category.
📐 Baseline & Proportional Ink
Bar charts must start at zero; area must match data value.
  • Truncated y-axes exaggerate differences and mislead viewers.
  • Bubble charts: encode by area (not radius) to avoid quadratic distortion.
  • Line charts are exempt from the zero baseline because position/judge slope is the encoding.
⚠️ A 2x radius increase = 4x area – always check proportional ink.
📢 4. Practical & Communication How we apply
🎯 Purpose-Driven Design
Align chart type, detail level, and interaction with goal & audience.
  • Exploratory analysis: high-density plots, interactive filters, raw numbers.
  • Explanatory presentation: simplified, annotated, with a clear takeaway title.
  • CEO report: 3 KPIs max. Analyst dashboard: drillable, multi-faceted.
👔 Know your audience: executive summary vs. deep-dive exploration.
♿ Accessibility & Inclusivity
Design for color-vision deficiency, screen readers, and low contrast.
  • Avoid red-green alone; use blue-orange or add texture/patterns.
  • Ensure text meets WCAG AA contrast ratio (4.5:1 for normal text).
  • Provide descriptive alt-text and enable keyboard navigation.
🦯 Test with CVD simulator; never rely solely on color to convey meaning.
📰 Glance-and-Go Narrative
Communicate the key message within 5 seconds using active titles.
  • Replace generic titles ("Sales by Month") with insight-driven ones ("Sales dipped 12% in March").
  • Integrate annotations directly onto data points to guide the viewer.
  • Use bold, large text for the main finding; smaller, neutral text for axes.
💬 “Marketing spend is flat, but cost-per-lead is rising sharply.”
🖱️ 5. Interactive & System How we explore
👆 Direct Manipulation
Click, brush, or drag data points to filter and explore directly.
  • Brushing a scatter plot immediately updates linked visualizations.
  • Avoid separate control panels when possible; let the data be the interface.
  • Immediate visual feedback (less than 100ms) maintains the user's flow state.
🔄 Click a bar → filter map; drag a range → zoom timeline.
📱 Responsive Visual Complexity
Adapt chart detail and layout to screen size, not just scaling.
  • Desktop: full scatter plot with hover tooltips; Mobile: sparkline + key number.
  • Rearrange dashboard tiles into a single column on narrow viewports.
  • Touch targets should be at least 44x44px for mobile interactivity.
📲 Mobile shows aggregated KPI; tap to expand trend.
🔽 Progressive Disclosure
Reveal complexity on demand; keep the initial view simple.
  • Start with high-level summary; offer "drill-down" paths for deeper data.
  • Use accordions, expandable sections, or hierarchical navigation.
  • Reduces cognitive overload for casual users while serving power analysts.
🧅 Global revenue → click continent → country detail → city table.
