📋 Bank Loan Risk Analysis

Brainstorming on Analytical Prototyping for Loan Default Risk Monitoring and Mitigation
with an Emphasis on Visual Design for Subsequent Data and Modeling Storytelling
📁 Dataset Overview metadata
📌 Sample size
1,000 loan records (as per PDF reference)
🔢 Variables & types
Numerical: Credit_score, Amount, Checking_amount, Saving_amount, Emp_duration, Age, Term
Categorical: Gender, Marital_status, Emp_status, Car_loan, Personal_loan, Home_loan, Education_loan
Binary target: Default (0/1)
📅 Data collection context
Hypothetical bank loan application records – includes credit bureau scores, loan characteristics, existing debt flags, and employment/demographic information. Suitable for prototyping early warning systems and portfolio segmentation.
🧠 Q1: What drives default most? predictor strength
“Which customer attributes (credit score, loan amount, balances, tenure) are the strongest indicators of future default?”
🎯 Motivation
To build a simple, rule‑based early warning system. The bank needs to focus underwriting and monitoring on the top 3‑5 drivers, reducing complexity and improving interpretability.
📊 Recommended visuals
Correlation heatmap (numeric variables vs Default), box plots segmented by Default, bar chart of standardized coefficients (from logistic regression).
⚙️ Base R graphical functions
heatmap(), cor(), boxplot(), barplot(), plot()
📐 Suggested analytical methods
Logistic RegressionRandom ForestCorrelation AnalysisANOVA (for categorical comparisons)
👥 Q2: How do demographics and job status affect risk? segmentation
“Do age, employment duration, marital status, or self‑employment reveal distinct default patterns? Which segments need stricter policies?”
🎯 Motivation
Tailored risk tiers and income verification strategies. For instance, self‑employed or young professionals with short tenure may require additional scrutiny or different product offers.
📊 Recommended visuals
Stacked bar charts (normalized to 100%) for categorical variables, density overlays for Age and Emp_duration, grouped bar charts for average credit score by two dimensions.
⚙️ Base R graphical functions
barplot(..., beside = FALSE), density(), lines(), plot(), aggregate() + barplot(..., beside = TRUE)
📐 Suggested analytical methods
Chi‑square test of independencet‑test / Wilcoxon testLogistic regression with interaction terms
🔗 Q3: Do overlapping loans raise default risk? debt intersection
“How does the number and combination of existing loans (car, personal, home, education) affect default probability? Which pairings are most dangerous?”
🎯 Motivation
Refine credit policy by capping total exposure for risky combinations (e.g., personal + education loan) and identifying “thin‑file” risk (zero existing loans). Helps set exposure limits.
📊 Recommended visuals
Grouped bar chart of default rate by total number of active loans (0 to 4), heatmap crossing two binary loan types (e.g., Home_loan vs Car_loan) with default rate as color.
⚙️ Base R graphical functions
barplot(), image() or filled.contour(), table(), apply() for aggregation, legend()
📐 Suggested analytical methods
Multivariate frequency analysisUpset or UpSet plot (alternative to Venn, first introduced in 2014))Logistic regression with interaction terms
⚖️ Q4: How to set loan terms and liquidity requirements? product design
“Do high‑amount, long‑term loans produce a ‘danger zone’ of defaults? Do low checking/saving balances signal liquidity stress?”
🎯 Motivation
Design dynamic limits (amount × term) based on credit score and minimum balance requirements. The bank can reject or downgrade applications that fall into the danger zone unless offset by strong credit quality.
📊 Recommended visuals
Scatter plot Amount vs Term colored by Default; bubble plot with Credit_score as bubble size; side‑by‑side box plots for Checking_amount and Saving_amount by Default status.
⚙️ Base R graphical functions
plot(), points(), symbols() or plot(..., cex = ), boxplot(), legend()
📐 Suggested analytical methods
Bivariate outlier detectionLogistic regression (amount × term interaction)Threshold optimisation
📉 Q5: How to monitor portfolio risk over time? score & threshold
“What is the overall risk distribution of the current portfolio? How to choose an intervention threshold and detect ‘risk creep’?”
🎯 Motivation
Implement a composite risk score (e.g., predicted probability from logistic model) for proactive account management. Track shifts in the density plot to catch deteriorating underwriting standards.
📊 Recommended visuals
Density plot of predicted default probabilities (full portfolio), gauge/bar chart of overall default rate vs benchmark (e.g., 5%), density overlay after simulated policy tightening.
⚙️ Base R graphical functions
density(), plot(), lines(), barplot(), abline(), quantile() for threshold, hist() (alternative)
📐 Suggested analytical methods
Logistic regression (probability scoring)ROC / AUC evaluationCalibration plotTime‑series risk tracking (if temporal data available)
